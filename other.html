MCM

The Mathematical Competition in Modeling (MCM) is an annual competition hosted by the Consortium for Mathematics and its Applications (COMAP). The goal of the competition is to solve one of two provided problems and write a paper-like solution to the chosen problem. Below are the details of the two years I participated in the competition.

Please be mindful of the fact that these solutions were prepared over the course of only four days, so there will be typos and incomplete sections to our solutions.

2016 - A Hot Bath

A collaboration with Nathan Mull and Stephen Lu. This year we submitted a solution to Problem A. Here's a quick summary of the problem:

A person fills up a bathtub with hot water. Since the tub will naturally cool down and have an uneven temperature throughout, develop a model of the bathtub water and use that model to come up with a strategy to help the person keep the original temperature they filled the tub with and the water temperature as even throughout the tub as possible, without wasting too much water.

This is our full, unedited [solution]. On a high-level, our solution is to model the bath tub with a set of PDE's and find the approximate solution to the boundary value problem with the finite element method (FEM). Then, using this approximate solution, use a hybrid model of the bath-taking experience to generate optimal control for a user with control over the faucet flow rate, faucet temperature, and moving to the front and back of the tub.

The toolboxes we used were MATLAB's PDE Toolbox and the Hybrid Toolbox for MATLAB.

2015 - Searching for a lost plane

A collaboration with Nathan Mull and Stephen Lu. This year we submitted a solution to Problem B. Here's the problem statement (from the MCM website):

Recall the lost Malaysian flight MH370. Build a generic mathematical model that could assist "searchers" in planning a useful search for a lost plane feared to have crashed in open water such as the Atlantic, Pacific, Indian, Southern, or Arctic Ocean while flying from Point A to Point B. Assume that there are no signals from the downed plane. Your model should recognize that there are many different types of planes for which we might be searching and that there are many different types of search planes, often using different electronics or sensors. Additionally, prepare a 1-2 page non-technical paper for the airlines to use in their press conferences concerning their plan for future searches.

This our full, unedited [solution]. Our solution to this problem is a formulation for a probabilistic simulation of the plane's location. The idea is that by running this simulation which was built on our model of the dynamics of both a plane in the air and floating in the ocean search pilots could begin their searches from high probability peak locations.
